In the News and on the Web

Below is a collection of featured topics and articles related to Prof. Hareli's research and the field of social psychology of emotions.

Featured Topics and Articles

Abandoned Doctors Felt Slighted

Coverage and discussion of research on how emotional expressions in doctor-patient relationships impact perceptions and outcomes.

Social Emotions

Featured articles discussing the nature and function of social emotions in interpersonal and organizational contexts.

When to Scowl

Research findings on how anger expressions influence credibility and social perceptions, challenging conventional wisdom about emotion display.

Dominance Expression Conveyed By Different Facial Expressions In Men And Women

Examination of how gender influences the perception of dominance and social power based on emotional expressions.

Failure, Emotions, and Explaining It to Your Boss

Coverage of research on how emotional expressions affect the effectiveness of explanations for failure in workplace settings.

Emotionally Restrained Women Are Seen as Less Intelligent I

First in a series examining gender stereotypes and how emotional restraint affects perceptions of competence and intelligence.

Emotionally Restrained Women Are Viewed as Less Intelligent II

Follow-up research confirming the paradoxical effect of emotional restraint on competence perceptions across different contexts.

Emotions and Situative Informativeness

Exploration of the concept of situative informativeness—how different emotions vary in their susceptibility to contextual influences and meaning.

How to Decide Who Will Help Us?

Research on how emotional expressions and facial features influence perceptions of helpfulness and the likelihood of receiving social support.

Research Impact

Prof. Hareli's research on the social perception of emotions has been featured in various academic and popular media outlets. His work addresses fundamental questions about how we judge others based on their emotional expressions and how these judgments affect social interactions, workplace dynamics, and interpersonal relationships.

The research demonstrates that emotional expressions serve important social functions, communicating not just internal states but also social information that influences how others perceive us—including judgments about our competence, trustworthiness, dominance, and willingness to help.
